A futarchy equilibrium atlas

Bayes-Nash equilibria across mechanism variants and player taxonomies, computed via OpenSpiel CFR+/MCCFR.

Working draft · May 2026 · Source: futarchy-fi/simulations/mechanism-design

Scope

Futarchy proposals have proliferated faster than rigorous analysis of their equilibria. Most arguments for or against a particular mechanism are made informally, anchored on intuition or on small-scale experiments with LLM or human traders. Recent work by Galanis (arXiv:2604.20050) is a clean exception: it experimentally tests whether LLM-driven prediction markets aggregate information across four structures of increasing complexity. The empirical finding is that information aggregation degrades sharply with complexity — but the paper does not solve the underlying game, so the source of that degradation is unclear: are the LLM markets failing because the equilibrium itself fails to aggregate, or because the LLMs are far from any equilibrium?

This atlas answers that question for several mechanism families. For each (mechanism × player mix × complexity) cell, we solve the underlying extensive-form game with OpenSpiel's tabular CFR+ (or MCCFR where state-space size demands it), characterise the equilibrium price/decision distribution, and quantify the surplus available to a capital-rich manipulator. Where comparable empirical data exists, we contrast the equilibrium with what current LLM traders actually do.

Method, in one paragraph

Each mechanism is encoded as a pyspiel.Game: chance nodes draw the private signals, players act sequentially against a market-maker or auctioneer with discretised action spaces, and terminal payoffs are computed from the realised resolution. We solve each game with OpenSpiel's tabular CFR+ and track NashConv (sum of per-player best-response improvements over the current average policy) as the convergence signal. NashConv ≤ 2 × 10⁻⁴ is treated as converged; this is below the discretisation noise floor in every game we report. Caveat: for three-or-more-player general-sum games, CFR+ converges formally to a coarse correlated equilibrium, not a Nash equilibrium; we argue per-mechanism that the gap is negligible for the games we consider, because each mechanism provides a strictly positive-EV unilateral deviation that rules out babbling-style CCEs.

Chapter 1 The Galanis market

The question

Galanis (2026) introduces a sequential prediction market with three traders and four information-and-payoff structures of strictly increasing complexity. Each structure is separable: by a theorem of Ostrovsky (2012), there exists a Bayes-Nash equilibrium in which the market price converges to the truth in finitely many rounds. Galanis runs the markets with LLM traders and observes that aggregation degrades sharply with complexity: median final price drops from ≈ 0.98 in the easy structures to ≈ 0.5 (coin toss) in the Very Hard structure, even though every structure admits an aggregating equilibrium in principle.

The open question Galanis does not resolve: does the equilibrium itself degrade with complexity, or are the LLMs simply not playing the equilibrium? Ostrovsky guarantees existence of one aggregating equilibrium — there can be others that fail to aggregate. Without solving the game we cannot disentangle these two hypotheses.

What we did

  • Encoded the Galanis game as a pyspiel.Game: three players in fixed rotation, a uniform-prior chance node over the 8 possible signal triples, partitions implementing each of the four structures, and LMSR pricing (liquidity β = 0.01) with a discretised grid of 11 target prices.
  • Solved with OpenSpiel's tabular CFR+ (50 iterations per structure, ≈ 2 min each on a laptop CPU).
  • Computed NashConv as a convergence diagnostic, then derived the distribution of final prices conditional on each chance outcome under the average policy.
  • Compared against (a) the empirical numbers reported in the paper and (b) a closed-form myopic Bayes-Nash analytical benchmark.

What this means

Headline. The equilibrium aggregates information in all four structures. The empirical scaling of log error with complexity (0.018 → 0.018 → 0.304 → 0.718) is not a property of the equilibrium — it is a capability gap between current LLMs and best-response play.

Galanis frames the empirical scaling as suggesting that AI agents may suffer the same limitations as humans when reasoning about what others know as complexity grows. The equilibrium computation here shows that this interpretation conflates two distinct claims: (a) the equilibrium of the Very Hard market does not aggregate, versus (b) the LLM markets do not reach the equilibrium. Our result is that (a) is false — best-response play aggregates in every structure, consistent with Ostrovsky's separability theorem — and therefore the empirical scaling is evidence for (b). The path of improvement is in trader capability (correct Bayesian updating, correct higher-order belief reasoning), not in mechanism design: the LMSR mechanism is doing its job.

Why CFR median is larger than empirical median on Easy/Medium

The CFR median log error of 0.087 is larger than the empirical 0.018 on the easy structures. This is a discretisation artefact: our 11-action grid caps target prices at 0.917, imposing a log-error floor of −log(0.917) ≈ 0.087. LLM traders use effectively continuous prices and can reach ≈ 0.99, hence the lower empirical log error on the easy structures. The fair qualitative reading is: both aggregate near-perfectly when the structure admits it.

Caveats specific to this chapter

  • CCE vs Nash. Tabular CFR+ in a three-player general-sum game converges to a coarse correlated equilibrium, not a Nash equilibrium. For the Galanis game the gap between CCE and NE is small: the LMSR market maker creates a strictly positive-EV unilateral deviation for any non-aggregating profile, which rules out babbling-style CCEs. NashConv ≤ 2 × 10⁻⁴ at termination in every run.
  • Three rounds only. The Galanis paper studies 3, 6, and 9 rounds. Tabular CFR's information-state count scales as cells × actionsmoves_until_player_acts_again; at 9 rounds × 11 actions × Very Hard structure this is ≈ 1.7 × 10⁹ — well outside tabular Python. We solve the 3-round game; MCCFR or a C++ port is required for longer horizons and is left as follow-up.
  • Discretisation grid. 11 actions is the binding floor on log error. A finer grid (21 or 99 actions) lowers the floor but is heavier to solve; the qualitative aggregation result is unchanged.

Chapter 3 Amoveo insurance-style futarchy

In preparation. Single binary market on "will X happen", with the decision going to the less-likely side. Designed to flatten manipulation cost across outcomes: if both outcomes are roughly equally likely a priori, moving the price in either direction costs symmetrically. The equilibrium question: what does the equilibrium look like when traders know the decision rule rewards the underdog side?

Chapter 6 Player taxonomy and manipulation surplus

In preparation. We hold the mechanism fixed and vary the player population: myopic Bayesian, strategic best-responder, budget-constrained manipulator (with explicit attacker budget B), informed insider with high-precision signal, naive flat-prior trader, and LLM agents instantiated for empirical anchoring. The headline metric is the manipulation surplus M(B): the equilibrium price/decision shift achievable by spending B. We expect this to differ sharply across mechanisms — in particular, Amoveo's insurance design should make M(B) flatter at the cost of weaker aggregation when one side is strongly favoured.
